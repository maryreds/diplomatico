Mexico's semiconductor industry is experiencing accelerating growth, with the market expanding from USD 10.4 billion in 2024 to a projected USD 18.4 billion by 2033, driven by nearshoring momentum and a deepening electronics manufacturing base. The country's geographic proximity to the United States, USMCA trade advantages, and a pipeline of 110,000–130,000 engineering graduates annually make it a compelling alternative to Asian supply chains. However, Mexico remains structurally dependent on imports—exceeding USD 23.5 billion annually—and lacks domestic fabrication and OSAT facilities, defining both the scale of the challenge and the magnitude of the investment opportunity.

Política Gubernamental e Incentivos
January 2025
Presidential Decree on Semiconductor Tax Incentives
President Sheinbaum issued a presidential decree granting enhanced tax deductions for capital equipment investment and employee training costs specifically targeting semiconductor nearshoring operations. The measure is designed to close the fiscal competitiveness gap between Mexico and rival destinations such as India and Vietnam.
February 2025
Launch of the Kutsari National Semiconductor Initiative
Named from the Purépecha word for silicon, Kutsari establishes a National Semiconductor Design Center with nodes in Jalisco, Puebla, and Sonora, paired with an accelerated designer training program and fast-track patent registration reforms. Phase 1 (2024–2027) focuses on design centers and chip design hubs, while Phase 2 (2027–2030) targets wafer fabrication at mature process nodes.
Ongoing
Plan Mexico — Semiconductor Export Doubling Target
The federal government's Plan Mexico sets a national goal to double both semiconductor exports and sector employment by 2030, providing the strategic framework under which Kutsari and the tax incentive regime operate. This overarching plan signals multi-year policy continuity to prospective foreign investors.
Ongoing
Chip Design Park — Zapopan, Jalisco
Jalisco state government is developing a dedicated Chip Design Park in Zapopan, funded with an initial 18 million pesos and scaling to a 50 million peso permanent facility, targeting the incubation of at least 10 semiconductor design startups by 2030. The park is intended to anchor the Kutsari ecosystem in Mexico's most advanced semiconductor state.
Oportunidades Bilaterales México–Malasia
Mexico and Malaysia occupy complementary positions in the global semiconductor value chain—Mexico excelling in chip design and advanced assembly, Malaysia commanding approximately 13% of global OSAT capacity and packaging roughly 26% of all US-destined chips. Both nations are CPTPP members with 50 years of diplomatic relations, creating a ready-made preferential trade and investment framework to formalize and deepen this structural complementarity.
OSAT Capacity Transfer to Mexico
Malaysian OSAT leaders such as Inari Amertron, Unisem, and Greatech are well-positioned to establish Mexico-based packaging and test facilities that would fill Mexico's most critical structural gap. A Malaysian-operated OSAT plant in Jalisco or Baja California would simultaneously serve US customers under USMCA and leverage Mexico's existing design talent pipeline.
CPTPP Trade Framework Optimization
As co-members of the Comprehensive and Progressive Agreement for Trans-Pacific Partnership, Mexico and Malaysia can leverage preferential tariff schedules to rebalance a currently lopsided bilateral semiconductor trade relationship—Malaysia exports USD 6.03 billion in integrated circuits to Mexico versus only USD 151 million flowing the other way. Structured bilateral trade missions and investment matchmaking under CPTPP auspices represent a near-term, low-friction mechanism to grow two-way flows.
Joint Semiconductor Workforce Development
Malaysia's Penang Skills Development Centre model and its established semiconductor training ecosystem offer a proven template that Mexico's Kutsari accelerated training program could adopt or co-develop. A formal bilateral skills-exchange agreement would accelerate Mexico's ambition to produce 3,000 chip design engineers in Jalisco alone by 2030.
Co-Investment in Mature-Node Fabrication
Mexico's Phase 2 Kutsari target of domestic wafer fabrication at mature process nodes by 2030 aligns with Malaysia's expertise in trailing-edge fab operations. Malaysian technology partners or government-linked companies could co-invest in Mexico's first fab, sharing capital risk while securing preferential access to the USMCA market.
Factores de Riesgo
⚠️ No Domestic Fabrication or OSAT Capability
Mexico currently has zero semiconductor fabs and zero OSAT facilities, meaning the country remains entirely dependent on foreign partners for the most value-intensive steps of the semiconductor supply chain. Until Kutsari Phase 2 materializes post-2027, Mexico's value-add is structurally limited to design and final assembly.
⚠️ USMCA 2026 Review Uncertainty
The scheduled 2026 USMCA joint review introduces potential renegotiation risk that could alter rules-of-origin requirements, tariff schedules, or investment protections that currently underpin Mexico's nearshoring value proposition. Investors with long capital payback cycles—such as fab operators—face elevated policy uncertainty during this window.
⚠️ Infrastructure Constraints: Water and Energy
Several of Mexico's key semiconductor states face documented constraints in water availability and energy reliability, both of which are critical inputs for semiconductor manufacturing and data center operations. Without targeted infrastructure investment, these bottlenecks could cap the scale and type of operations that can be viably located in Mexico.
⚠️ Intensifying Global FDI Competition
Mexico faces direct competition for semiconductor foreign direct investment from Malaysia, Vietnam, India, and Poland—each offering aggressive incentive regimes, established OSAT ecosystems, or lower labor costs. Mexico must differentiate on USMCA access and proximity to US customers to remain competitive as these rival destinations continue to scale.
